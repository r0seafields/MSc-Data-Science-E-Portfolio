Unit 5 – Jaccard Distance/Dissimilarity

This unit focused on understanding the Jaccard coefficient and how it is used to measure similarity between observations containing binary data. The activity involved calculating the Jaccard similarity and Jaccard distance between three individuals using pathological test results before interpreting what the results represented.

Reflection

Unlike correlation or regression, which I had previously encountered during my BSc Psychology degree, the Jaccard coefficient was completely new to me. I initially found it one of the more difficult topics in the module because I struggled to understand exactly what the calculation was measuring and why shared absences were ignored. At first, the formula seemed quite abstract and I found it difficult to connect the mathematics with its practical application.

To overcome this, I worked back through the completed calculations and compared each patient's characteristics step by step. Reverse engineering the solution helped me understand how the coefficient only considers shared positive characteristics, making it particularly useful for binary datasets where the presence of a feature is more informative than its absence. Once I understood this concept, the calculation itself became much clearer.

The exercise demonstrated that Jack and Jim were the most similar pair, while Jim and Mary were the most dissimilar based on their test results. More importantly, it showed how selecting an appropriate similarity measure depends on the type of data being analysed. I can now see how the Jaccard coefficient would be useful in healthcare applications for comparing patients based on their symptoms or test results, as well as in clustering and classification problems where binary data is common.

Although this was one of the more challenging activities in the module, overcoming that initial difficulty has improved my confidence when approaching unfamiliar machine learning concepts. Rather than simply accepting a result, I learned the importance of understanding why a particular metric is appropriate and what the output actually tells us. Looking back, this activity reinforced that persistence and revisiting difficult concepts are often necessary parts of the learning process.
