Unit 1 – Collaborative Discussion: 4th Industrial Revolution

Machine Learning Module

Collaborative Discussion Post

Industry 4.0 has transformed healthcare through technologies such as electronic health records (EHRs), artificial intelligence (AI), cloud computing and connected medical systems. Xu, Xu and Li (2018) describe Industry 4.0 as integrating digital technologies to improve automation and data-driven decision-making. Metcalf, Nousala and Ing (2024) suggest that Industry 5.0 builds on this by focusing on resilience and ensuring technology supports people rather than replacing them.

A recent example of an information system failure is the 2024 Synnovis ransomware attack, which disrupted pathology services across several NHS hospitals in London. According to NHS England (2024), the attack delayed blood tests, surgeries and outpatient appointments because clinicians could not access laboratory results. This had a direct impact on patient safety, as delayed diagnoses and treatment decisions can negatively affect patient outcomes.

This incident shows that while Industry 4.0 technologies improve efficiency, they also increase dependence on digital systems. Industry 5.0 highlights the importance of resilient, secure systems that enable healthcare professionals to continue delivering safe patient care during cyber incidents. I believe this demonstrates that cybersecurity should be considered a core component of patient safety, rather than just an IT issue.

Reflection

For this collaborative discussion, I researched the impact of Industry 4.0 and Industry 5.0 on healthcare, using the 2024 Synnovis cyberattack as an example of how information system failures can affect patient safety. Through this activity, I developed a stronger understanding of how technologies such as cloud computing, artificial intelligence and interconnected information systems can improve healthcare services while also introducing new cybersecurity risks.

Drawing on the work of Metcalf, Nousala and Ing (2024) and Xu, Xu and Li (2018), I recognised that the transition from Industry 4.0 to Industry 5.0 is not only about technological advancement but also about designing systems that are resilient, secure and centred on human needs.

Although I did not submit my discussion within the required timeframe and was therefore unable to participate in the peer response and summary stages, I reviewed my peers' published summary posts to reflect on the wider learning from the activity. Their reflections broadened my understanding beyond my initial research.

Larussa (2026) highlighted the importance of governance frameworks, including the NHS Data Security and Protection requirements and the Caldicott Principles, in strengthening cybersecurity within healthcare organisations. Illinic (2026) reinforced the need for resilient system design, human oversight and fail-safe mechanisms, demonstrating that systems should be designed to minimise disruption when failures occur. Jackson (2026) also emphasised that cybersecurity failures in sectors such as healthcare are not solely technical or financial issues but also ethical ones, as they can directly affect patient safety, privacy and public trust.

Reviewing these perspectives reinforced my own conclusion that successful digital transformation depends not only on adopting advanced technologies but also on implementing robust governance, cybersecurity and resilience measures. This activity also highlighted the value of collaborative learning, as exposure to different viewpoints encouraged me to consider aspects of the topic that I had not initially explored.

In future modules, I will ensure I engage with discussion activities within the required timescales so that I can contribute to peer learning, receive constructive feedback and further develop my critical thinking through academic discussion.
